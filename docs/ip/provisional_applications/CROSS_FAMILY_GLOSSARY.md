## PURPOSE

## AUTHORITATIVE CROSS-FAMILY DEFINITIONS

### "Admissibility" / "Admissible"

**Uniform Definition (all three families):**
The property of a proposed automated action or decision of satisfying all applicable pre-execution requirements — governance, compliance, and identity — such that the action is permitted to proceed to execution commitment. Admissibility is a binary property at the moment of execution commitment: a proposed action is either admissible (all pre-execution requirements satisfied) or inadmissible (one or more pre-execution requirements not satisfied). Admissibility is evaluated at the execution boundary — the computational point at which a proposed action would transition from a recommendation into an irreversible real-world action — and not at any earlier point in the decision generation process.

**Usage in each family:**
- **Family 1 (OMNIX-PAT-2026-001):** A proposed action is admissible when it has received APPROVE verdicts from all checkpoints in the sequential pipeline. The DCI CONTRADICTORY classification renders a proposed action inadmissible regardless of the individual signal values that motivated it.
- **Family 2 (OMNIX-PAT-2026-002):** The Non-Markovian Memory Kernel and CAES generate regime-calibrated decision parameters that are submitted for admissibility evaluation by the governance pipeline. High regime confidence does not independently confer admissibility; it contributes to the admissibility evaluation as one of multiple independent signal sources.
- **Family 3 (OMNIX-PAT-2026-003):** A proposed transaction is admissible only when both Sharia compliance clearance and dual-layer authorization (voice biometric + PQC signature) have been successfully completed. Admissibility in this context requires satisfaction of both ethical compliance requirements and identity verification requirements independently.

### "Checkpoint"

**Uniform Definition (all three families):**
A discrete computational evaluation unit within an automated pre-execution enforcement pipeline that: (a) receives one or more governance signals as input; (b) evaluates said signals against predetermined or dynamically computed criteria; (c) issues a verdict of PASS or BLOCK (or equivalent binary outcome); and (d) has independent authority to prevent the proposed action from proceeding to execution by issuing a BLOCK verdict, regardless of the verdicts of any other checkpoint in the pipeline. A checkpoint does not aggregate its result with the results of other checkpoints; it operates independently and its BLOCK verdict is individually sufficient to prevent execution.

**Structural properties (all families):**
- Independence: Each checkpoint operates without awareness of the evaluation results of other checkpoints at the time of its own evaluation.
- Veto authority: A BLOCK verdict from any single checkpoint is independently sufficient to prevent execution.
- Fail-closed default: If a checkpoint is unable to evaluate its assigned inputs, the default verdict is BLOCK. Uncertainty defaults to prevention, not permission.
- Auditability: Each checkpoint records its evaluation in the decision trace with sufficient detail to support post-hoc audit and regulatory review.

**Usage note:** The term "checkpoint" as used in these applications refers specifically to a software-implemented computational evaluation unit within a pre-execution governance pipeline. It does not refer to a network checkpoint, a model checkpoint (in the machine learning sense), or a physical security checkpoint.

### "Decision" / "Proposed Decision" / "Proposed Action"

**Uniform Definition (all three families):**
An instruction or recommendation generated by an automated system (including but not limited to a machine learning model, an algorithmic trading engine, a credit scoring system, or any automated recommendation system) that, if permitted to proceed, would cause a change in the real world (including but not limited to the execution of a financial transaction, the approval of a credit application, the triggering of an insurance claim, or any other irreversible commitment of resources or obligations). The terms "decision," "proposed decision," and "proposed action" are used interchangeably in these applications and refer to this same concept.

**Critical distinction:** A "decision" or "proposed action" as used herein refers to the output of the decision generation subsystem that has been submitted for governance evaluation. It does not refer to the governance verdict itself (which is designated as APPROVE, HOLD, or BLOCK). The governance system evaluates proposed decisions; it does not itself make decisions in the substantive sense.

---

### "Execution Boundary"

**Uniform Definition (all three families):**
The computational point in a system's processing pipeline at which a proposed action transitions from a recommendation or intent into an instruction transmitted to an execution environment that will cause an irreversible real-world action. The execution boundary is the final point at which governance enforcement, compliance evaluation, and identity authorization can prevent execution. Once a proposed action has crossed the execution boundary, it has been committed and cannot be recalled by the governance system.

---

### "Decision Trace" / "Audit Receipt"

**Uniform Definition (all three families):**
A structured data record documenting the complete evaluation history of a proposed action through the pre-execution enforcement pipeline, including: all governance signals evaluated, all checkpoint verdicts with reasons, all compliance evaluation results, all authentication events, the final admissibility determination, and a cryptographic integrity seal. The terms "decision trace" and "audit receipt" refer to the same type of structured record; "decision trace" is used in the context of governance checkpoint evaluation (Family 1), while "audit receipt" is used in the context of the combined compliance and authorization record (Family 3). In all cases, the record is cryptographically sealed using a post-quantum digital signature algorithm (Dilithium-3 / ML-DSA-65, NIST FIPS 204) to provide tamper-evidence.

---

### "Post-Quantum" / "Quantum-Resistant"

**Uniform Definition (all three families):**
Referring to cryptographic algorithms and protocols that are not known to be efficiently solvable by a quantum computer using currently known quantum algorithms, including Shor's algorithm (applicable to RSA and ECC) and Grover's algorithm (applicable to symmetric key search). As used in these applications, "post-quantum" and "quantum-resistant" are synonymous and refer specifically to algorithms that have been standardized or are under standardization by the United States National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) Post-Quantum Cryptography Standardization process, including ML-DSA (Dilithium, NIST FIPS 204) and ML-KEM (Kyber, NIST FIPS 203).
